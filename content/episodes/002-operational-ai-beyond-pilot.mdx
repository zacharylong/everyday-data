**Patricia:** Today we're talking about something I think every person in enterprise data has either experienced directly or watched happen to a team nearby — the AI pilot that never ships.

**Zac:** The proof-of-concept purgatory.

**Patricia:** Exactly. You do the pilot. It works. People are excited. The demo goes great. And then six months later you're asking what happened to that project and no one really has a clean answer.

**Zac:** And I think the first thing we need to name is that this isn't a technology problem. The models work. The use case is real. Something in the translation from experiment to production keeps breaking.

**Patricia:** Let's start with how pilots are typically designed, because I think that's where the gap originates. When you're running a pilot, you're optimizing for learning and for demonstrating value. You're not optimizing for reliability.

**Zac:** Right. You pick a slice of clean data. You define the scope tightly. You have someone very capable running things by hand. The conditions are ideal.

**Patricia:** And then production is... none of that. Data is messy. The scope expands. The capable person who ran the pilot has moved on. And now you need the system to work unsupervised, reliably, every day.

**Zac:** Infrastructure is one of the first things that breaks. What's the data pipeline that keeps this system fed with fresh, quality data? In a pilot, maybe someone was manually refreshing a CSV. That doesn't work in production.

**Patricia:** And then monitoring. How do you know if the model is degrading? How do you know if the outputs are drifting from what you'd expect? In a pilot, a person is looking at results carefully. In production, you need automated signals.

**Zac:** The ownership question is the one that I think gets underappreciated the most. Who is accountable when the AI system produces a wrong answer at 2am on a Tuesday?

**Patricia:** In so many organizations, that question doesn't have a clean answer. The data science team built it. The engineering team deployed it. The business team uses it. Nobody owns it.

**Zac:** And that diffusion of ownership is lethal for operational systems. Somebody has to be on the hook.

**Patricia:** I've seen organizations where they actually created explicit ML system owners — people whose job description includes being accountable for specific models in production. And those are the organizations where things actually stay working.

**Zac:** Let's talk about what the organizations that do ship AI consistently have in common. Because they exist. This isn't impossible.

**Patricia:** The pattern I see is that they treat AI as infrastructure, not as innovation theater. They're not chasing the demo. They're asking from the beginning: what does it take to run this reliably for two years?

**Zac:** That question changes a lot of decisions. It changes what you build the first version on. It changes what monitoring you put in place. It changes how you structure the team.

**Patricia:** And it changes how you talk to stakeholders. Instead of "here's what AI can do," the conversation is "here's what it takes to make this operational, and here's what we need from the business side to get there."

**Zac:** That's a harder conversation. But it's the right one.

**Patricia:** The organizations that skip that conversation are the ones who end up with beautiful pilots and no operational capability.

**Zac:** Alright — practical recommendations. If someone is sitting in a meeting right now planning an AI pilot, what's the most important thing to do differently?

**Patricia:** Define what "operational" looks like before you start the pilot. What's the data pipeline? Who owns it? How will you monitor it? If you can't answer those questions, you're not ready to pilot.

**Zac:** And if you're already in purgatory with a pilot that worked and now isn't going anywhere — find out who owns it. If the answer is unclear, that's your problem to solve before anything technical.

**Patricia:** Well said. Thanks everyone for listening. Find us on Spotify, Apple Podcasts, and YouTube.
