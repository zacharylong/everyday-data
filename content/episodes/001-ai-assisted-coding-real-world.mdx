**Patricia:** Welcome back to Everyday Data. I'm Patricia, and I'm here with Zac, and today we're diving into something that has been in every Slack channel and every engineering retrospective for the past year — AI coding assistants.

**Zac:** Right, and what we want to do today is actually get past the demo reel. Because I think everyone has seen the fifteen-minute YouTube video where someone builds a full web app in an afternoon with AI. That's not really what it looks like when you're doing production work day in and day out.

**Patricia:** Exactly. And I think that's especially true in data roles. The experience of using Copilot as a data analyst or analytics engineer is really different from the experience of a frontend developer building a Todo app.

**Zac:** Let's start there. What does "AI-assisted" actually mean for someone in a data role?

**Patricia:** For me it started with SQL. I was writing some fairly repetitive transformations — window functions, conditional aggregations — and Copilot started finishing my thoughts in ways that were actually useful. Not perfect, but useful as a starting draft.

**Zac:** And that's the key word — draft. The shift I had to make mentally was treating the AI output as a draft that I'm editing, not an answer that I'm approving.

**Patricia:** That mental model matters so much. Because if you're in approval mode, you start missing things. Hallucinated column names. Logic that looks right but doesn't handle nulls correctly. Joins that silently drop rows.

**Zac:** And it happens confidently. That's what makes it tricky. The model doesn't say "I'm not sure about this." It just produces code.

**Patricia:** Which is why I think code review actually gets more important, not less, when AI is in the loop. You're reviewing more code, faster, and some of that code was produced without the author fully reasoning through it step by step.

**Zac:** Let's talk tools for a second. Copilot, Cursor, Claude — they're not the same product. What are the real differences from your experience?

**Patricia:** Copilot is deeply integrated into VS Code and is very good at inline completion. It knows your repo context better than most. But it's reactive — it completes what you started.

**Zac:** Cursor feels more like a collaborator. You can describe what you want in natural language and it'll do a pass on a file or across multiple files. It reasons more. But that also means it makes bigger decisions you have to check.

**Patricia:** Claude — especially in the API or in the Projects feature — is where I go for things that require actual reasoning. Writing a complex data model. Thinking through an architecture decision. Getting an explanation of why something is behaving unexpectedly.

**Zac:** And the skill that no one talks about enough is the quality of the prompt. If you can't describe what you want precisely, you're going to get approximate results.

**Patricia:** That's become a core analytical skill now. It's not just about syntax. It's about being clear enough about your problem that the model can make useful progress on it.

**Zac:** So what does this mean for what it means to be a good engineer or analyst? If the model handles more of the implementation?

**Patricia:** I think it shifts the value up the stack. Understanding the problem deeply. Knowing what architecture makes sense. Reviewing what the model produces critically. Those things matter more.

**Zac:** And honestly, I think that's a reasonable trade. The parts of the work that benefit most from AI are the parts that were often bottlenecks — first drafts, boilerplate, translation of logic into code.

**Patricia:** The parts that need human judgment are still there. They're just more clearly in focus.

**Zac:** Alright — where can people find us if they want to continue the conversation?

**Patricia:** You can find Everyday Data on Spotify, Apple Podcasts, and YouTube. Links in the show notes.

**Zac:** And we'd love to hear what your actual experience has been with AI coding tools. Send us a note. Until next time.
