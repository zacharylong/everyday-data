**Zac:** If you've spent any time in enterprise data, you've heard someone say "we need to be more data-driven." And if you've heard that phrase, you've also probably seen what happens next — which is usually a training program, some dashboard rollouts, maybe a data literacy initiative that generates a lot of enthusiasm for about three months.

**Patricia:** And then life goes back to normal.

**Zac:** Right. So today we want to dig into what actually works when organizations are serious about building data literacy. And I think we should start by defining terms, because "data literacy" means very different things to different people.

**Patricia:** It does. I've seen it used to mean everything from "everyone should know how to read a chart" to "we're going to teach all our analysts Python."

**Zac:** My working definition is: data literacy is the ability to engage with data in ways that improve your decision-making. It's not a technical credential. It's a functional capability.

**Patricia:** And that means it looks very different for a VP of Operations than it does for a junior analyst. The VP doesn't need to write SQL. But they do need to know what questions data can and can't answer, how to probe an analysis they receive, and how to create the conditions for their team to use data well.

**Zac:** That framing already rules out a lot of common approaches. Teaching SQL to executives is almost never the right answer.

**Patricia:** Let's talk about training programs specifically, because they're the first thing organizations reach for. What's wrong with them?

**Zac:** They're not wrong, they're just insufficient on their own. A training program can build awareness and skills. What it can't do is change the conditions that people work in every day. If someone learns how to use Looker in a training session but then they're never in a meeting where using Looker is expected, the skill fades.

**Patricia:** It's the environment that determines whether skills get used and reinforced. Training programs often ignore the environment.

**Zac:** Self-service tooling is another thing organizations reach for. Give everyone a dashboard. Give everyone access to the data warehouse. That'll make them more data-literate.

**Patricia:** And it sometimes does. But it also sometimes creates a different problem — a proliferation of reports that don't agree with each other. Metrics that have different definitions in different parts of the organization.

**Zac:** The self-service chaos problem. You've enabled access without enabling understanding, and now you have twenty different versions of the monthly revenue number.

**Patricia:** The organizations that avoid this tend to be the ones that pair self-service access with strong data governance and strong support. Someone whose job is to help business partners use the tools correctly, not just use them.

**Zac:** That actually brings me to what I think is the most underrated structural approach: embedding analytics people in business teams rather than centralizing them.

**Patricia:** It's the difference between "come to us with your data questions" and "we are part of your team and we understand your domain."

**Zac:** When you embed an analyst in a business team, that analyst learns the domain deeply enough to ask good questions proactively. And the business team learns from working alongside that person every day — not from a training session.

**Patricia:** The literacy transfer happens through proximity, not curriculum.

**Zac:** That's a good line. Write that down.

**Patricia:** What about measurement? How do you know if data literacy is actually improving in an organization?

**Zac:** Honestly, I think the leading indicators are behavioral, not test scores. Are people asking better questions in data reviews? Are they pushing back on analyses when something seems off, rather than just accepting the number? Are they citing data in decision documents?

**Patricia:** Are the analytics team's requests getting more specific and better scoped over time? That's a signal that business partners are learning to think more precisely about what they need.

**Zac:** The lagging indicator is decision quality. But that's hard to measure directly.

**Patricia:** Alright. For someone who's in the middle of a data literacy initiative right now and things aren't moving — what's the most important thing to focus on?

**Zac:** Find one business team, embed someone, and go deep rather than broad. A single success story with a close partner is more valuable than a broad training rollout that no one remembers.

**Patricia:** Build the proof of concept internally, just like we talked about with AI systems. Show what's possible in a bounded scope, then expand.
